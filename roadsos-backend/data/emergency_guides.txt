Emergency Guides — First-Aid and Emergency Response Instructions
================================================================

IMPORTANT: Always call 112 (National Emergency) or 108 (Ambulance) FIRST before administering first-aid.
These guides are to help you provide immediate assistance until professional medical help arrives.

=================================================================
GUIDE 1: ROAD ACCIDENT — INITIAL RESPONSE (DR-ABC Protocol)
=================================================================

Step 1 — DANGER: Check for danger to yourself and others (leaking fuel, oncoming traffic, fire). Do NOT become a victim yourself.
Step 2 — RESPONSE: Check if the victim is responsive. Tap shoulders firmly and shout "Are you okay?"
Step 3 — AIRWAY: If unconscious, gently tilt the head back and lift the chin to open the airway. Remove visible obstructions from mouth.
Step 4 — BREATHING: Look, listen, and feel for breathing for 10 seconds.
Step 5 — CIRCULATION (CPR): If no breathing detected, begin CPR immediately (see Guide 2).
Step 6 — Call 108 immediately. State your location (landmark, highway name, km marker).

DO NOT:
- Move the victim if spinal injury is suspected (especially after high-speed crash or fall from vehicle).
- Remove a helmet from a motorcyclist unless the airway is blocked and you cannot open it any other way.
- Give water or food to an unconscious or semi-conscious person.
- Remove any object impaled in the body.

=================================================================
GUIDE 2: CARDIOPULMONARY RESUSCITATION (CPR)
=================================================================

When to perform: Victim is unresponsive and not breathing normally.

FOR ADULTS (> 8 years):
1. Place victim on their back on a firm, flat surface.
2. Kneel beside the chest. Place the heel of one hand on the centre of the chest (lower half of breastbone/sternum).
3. Place your other hand on top, interlocking fingers.
4. Keep arms straight. Press down hard and fast — compress at least 5 cm deep.
5. Rate: 100–120 compressions per minute (rhythm: "Stayin' Alive" by Bee Gees).
6. After every 30 compressions, give 2 rescue breaths (if trained):
   - Tilt head back, lift chin, pinch nose, seal your mouth over theirs and breathe for 1 second — watch chest rise.
7. Continue 30:2 ratio until ambulance arrives, victim regains consciousness, or you are physically unable to continue.

FOR INFANTS (< 1 year):
1. Use 2 fingers (index and middle) on the centre of the chest just below the nipple line.
2. Compress 4 cm deep. Rate: 100–120 per minute.
3. Give 2 gentle puffs of air (just enough to see chest rise).

AED (Automated External Defibrillator): If available at the scene (many highways and expressways have them), switch it on and follow voice instructions — it will guide you step by step.

=================================================================
GUIDE 3: SEVERE BLEEDING CONTROL
=================================================================

1. Apply firm, direct pressure with a clean cloth, bandage, or clothing over the wound.
2. Do NOT remove the cloth if it soaks through — add more material on top and press harder.
3. If bleeding is from a limb and is life-threatening, apply a tourniquet 5–8 cm above the wound (above the joint). Note the time applied and inform paramedics.
4. Elevate the injured limb above heart level if no fracture is suspected.
5. Keep the victim warm and calm — shock can develop rapidly after blood loss.
6. Do NOT apply tourniquet on the neck, chest, or abdomen.
7. Internal bleeding signs: Distended/rigid abdomen, bruising, dizziness, pale clammy skin, rapid weak pulse. Lay victim flat, raise legs 30 cm (if no spinal injury), and call 108 immediately.

=================================================================
GUIDE 4: FRACTURES AND BONE INJURIES
=================================================================

Signs: Pain, swelling, deformity, inability to move the limb, bone visible through skin (open fracture).

1. Do NOT try to realign or straighten the broken bone.
2. Immobilise the fracture using a splint (rolled newspaper, thick cardboard, or a rigid stick) — pad it with soft material and bandage in place. Keep the joint above and below the fracture immobilised.
3. For an open fracture (bone through skin): Cover the wound with a clean dressing. Do NOT push the bone back.
4. Apply ice pack (wrapped in cloth) to reduce swelling — do not place ice directly on skin.
5. Check circulation (pulse, warmth, colour) below the fracture every 5 minutes.
6. Suspected spinal/neck fracture: Do NOT move the victim. Support the head and neck in the position found. Wait for paramedics with spinal board.
7. Suspected pelvic fracture: Do not move victim. Keep legs flat and together. Call 108 urgently.

=================================================================
GUIDE 5: BURNS — VEHICLE FIRE OR FUEL BURN
=================================================================

1. Stop the burning: Remove the victim from the fire source. Smother flames with a blanket or roll them on the ground. Call 101 (Fire) and 108 (Ambulance).
2. Cool the burn: Run cool (not cold) water over the burned area for at least 20 minutes. Do NOT use ice, butter, toothpaste, or any home remedies.
3. Remove jewellery, watches, and clothing near the burn ONLY if not stuck to skin.
4. Cover with a clean, non-fluffy material (cling wrap or clean plastic bag works well).
5. Do NOT break blisters.
6. Do NOT remove clothing stuck to the burn.
7. Keep the victim warm to prevent shock.
8. Severity guide:
   - First degree: Redness, pain only (sunburn-like) — cool water, no hospital needed unless large area.
   - Second degree: Blisters, intense pain — hospital required.
   - Third degree: White/charred skin, no pain (nerve damage) — life-threatening, call 108 immediately.
9. Burns to face, hands, feet, genitals, or over joints are always medical emergencies.

=================================================================
GUIDE 6: HEAD INJURY AND CONCUSSION
=================================================================

Signs: Loss of consciousness (even brief), confusion, vomiting, unequal pupils, blood/fluid from nose or ears, severe headache.

1. Keep the victim still — assume spinal injury if mechanism was high-speed crash.
2. Gently support the head and neck in the position found — do NOT flex or rotate.
3. If unconscious but breathing: Place in recovery position ONLY if no spinal injury suspected.
4. If vomiting: Carefully roll victim onto their side (log roll — keep spine aligned with at least 2 helpers).
5. Control any scalp bleeding with gentle pressure — do NOT apply firm pressure if skull fracture is suspected (depressed/crunchy feel).
6. Do NOT give aspirin or ibuprofen for pain (increases bleeding risk).
7. All head injuries with loss of consciousness must be evaluated at a hospital — call 108.

=================================================================
GUIDE 7: SHOCK — TRAUMATIC SHOCK MANAGEMENT
=================================================================

Signs: Pale, cold, clammy skin; rapid weak pulse; rapid breathing; confusion or agitation; extreme thirst; fainting.

1. Lay victim flat. Raise legs 20–30 cm (unless head, chest, spine, or leg injury is suspected).
2. Keep warm — cover with a blanket or jacket.
3. Do NOT give food or water by mouth.
4. Loosen tight clothing around neck and chest.
5. Reassure the victim — speak calmly and continuously.
6. Monitor pulse and breathing every 2 minutes.
7. Call 108 immediately — shock is life-threatening within minutes without medical intervention.

=================================================================
GUIDE 8: CHOKING — AIRWAY OBSTRUCTION
=================================================================

Mild choking (victim can cough/speak):
1. Encourage strong coughing — do not intervene.

Severe choking (victim cannot speak, turning blue):
1. Give 5 firm back blows between shoulder blades with the heel of your hand (victim leaning forward).
2. If not cleared: Give 5 abdominal thrusts (Heimlich Manoeuvre):
   - Stand behind victim. Place one fist above the navel. Grasp fist with other hand. Pull sharply inward and upward.
3. Alternate 5 back blows and 5 abdominal thrusts until object is cleared or victim loses consciousness.
4. If victim loses consciousness: Start CPR — each time you open the airway to give breaths, look for and remove any visible object.

FOR INFANTS (< 1 year): Use 5 back blows face-down on forearm + 5 chest thrusts (NOT abdominal thrusts).

=================================================================
GUIDE 9: DROWNING / WATER SUBMERSION (Flood/Waterlogged Roads)
=================================================================

1. Do NOT enter deep/fast-moving water yourself — use a rope, clothing, branch, or flotation device to pull victim out.
2. Once out: Check for breathing. If not breathing, start CPR immediately (do NOT waste time trying to drain water from lungs — it is a myth).
3. Even if victim appears to recover, take to hospital immediately — secondary drowning (delayed fluid build-up in lungs) can be fatal within 24 hours.
4. Keep the victim warm — hypothermia is a major risk after water submersion.

=================================================================
GUIDE 10: TYRE BURST — VEHICLE CONTROL EMERGENCY
=================================================================

1. DO NOT slam the brakes — this will cause the vehicle to spin.
2. Grip the steering wheel firmly with both hands.
3. Gently accelerate briefly to maintain forward momentum and regain control.
4. Gradually ease off the accelerator — let the vehicle slow naturally.
5. Gently steer toward the shoulder. Apply brakes softly only after speed has reduced to under 60 km/h.
6. Switch on hazard lights. Pull fully off the road before stopping.
7. Place emergency triangle 50 m behind the vehicle. Call for roadside assistance (1033 NHAI).

Golden Hour: The first 60 minutes after a traumatic injury are critical.
Immediate action and calling 108 can save lives. Do not wait.
